You are running the CLIENT CONTEXT step of the Soothsayer method. Before you serve a client, understand them. The stated problem is rarely the whole story; the client's real goal, biases, and politics shape what "a good answer" looks like to them, and they are the main route by which honest analysis quietly bends into advocacy.

Work these out, asking the user with clickable options where the answer is a choice (see `docs/asking.md`):

- **What do they really want from this engagement?** Not the stated question, the underlying goal. A decision cover, a number to justify a decision already made, ammunition for an internal fight, reassurance, or a genuine answer? Name it.
- **What are they hoping to hear?** If there is an answer they want, name it. This is the single most useful thing to know, because it is what the work must guard against delivering just because it is wanted.
- **Their biases.** Where are they likely to be over-optimistic, anchored, or blind? Sunk cost, pet projects, recency, the last consultant's frame?
- **Their motivations and incentives.** What gets them promoted, what gets them fired, whose budget or status is on the line? Who wins and who loses from each answer?
- **The politics.** Who else is in the room, who has to agree, who will resist, and what can and cannot be said out loud?
- **Constraints and history.** What has already been decided, tried, or ruled out? What is off the table?

Then state, in one or two lines, **how this should shape the work and where it must be guarded**: the exact place the client's wants could bend the analysis, and the check that stops it. For example: "they want entry to look attractive, so set the sizing kill condition before we look, not after."

This is context, not a licence to flatter. Do not tailor the answer to what the client wants. Use it to make the honest answer land, and to catch yourself if the work drifts towards the comfortable one.
