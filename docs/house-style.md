# Soothsayer house style — The Economist

All prose the harness produces or ships follows this guide: the pack,
`/synthesize` output, `/communicate` output, assertion and question text, and the
skill documents themselves. Code, identifiers and file paths are exempt.

Why this guide, for this tool: the Economist's rules punish the exact writing
that hides weak thinking — long words, passive verbs, jargon, clichés and
framework-shaped prose. That is the same thing `/partner-review` is told to score
down ("penalise polish"). So this style is not decoration. It is a quality gate.
`/synthesize` and `/communicate` load this file; `/partner-review` and `/inhouse`
score prose against it and flag breaches.

## First principle

Be clear. The Economist's aim is to be understood at once. Clear writing follows
clear thinking; muddy prose usually means the analysis is muddy too. If a
sentence is hard to write plainly, suspect the idea, not the words.

## Orwell's six rules (the guide adopts these)

1. Never use a metaphor, simile or figure of speech you are used to seeing in print.
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If you can cut a word, cut it.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use jargon, a foreign phrase or a scientific word if an everyday word will serve.
6. Break any of these rules sooner than write something barbarous.

## The rules that matter most here

- **Short sentences. Short paragraphs.** One idea each. If a sentence runs past
  two lines, cut it in two.
- **Active voice.** "The team missed the risk", not "the risk was missed".
- **Plain words.** Use, not utilise. Buy, not procure. Enough, not sufficient.
- **Cut filler.** "In order to" is "to". "At this point in time" is "now".
  Delete "very", "really", "key", "in terms of", "going forward".
- **Concrete over abstract.** Name the number, the person, the date. "Sales fell
  12% in June", not "there were headwinds affecting performance".
- **Few adjectives, fewer adverbs.** Let the facts carry the weight.
- **No journalese or consultant-speak.** Banned: leverage (verb), impact (verb),
  deep dive, synergy, ecosystem, holistic, paradigm, robust, best-in-class,
  low-hanging fruit, move the needle, at the end of the day, circle back, unpack,
  learnings — plus the AI-vocabulary set (delve, crucial, comprehensive, nuanced,
  multifaceted, underscore, landscape, tapestry, foster, showcase).
- **No hype.** Avoid unique, iconic, revolutionary, game-changing. Claims stand on
  evidence, not adjectives.
- **Don't be too clever.** No puns in headings, no rhetorical questions dressed as
  analysis.

## Conventions

- **Spelling: British.** -our (colour, behaviour), -re (centre, metre), -ise
  (organise, analyse), programme, licence (noun) / license (verb). Avoid
  Americanisms: "different from" not "different than".
- **Capitals: sparing.** Lower-case job titles standing alone. Capitalise a title
  only before a name.
- **Numbers:** words for zero to ten, figures for 11 up. Use m and bn — $5m, €2bn.
- **Punctuation:** no exclamation marks outside quotations. No serial (Oxford)
  comma. No em dashes; use a comma, a full stop or parentheses.
- **Acronyms:** spell out on first use unless very well known. No full points
  (USA, not U.S.A.).

## One worked example

Before (fails the guide): "Leveraging a holistic framework, we conducted a deep
dive that surfaced several key learnings which, going forward, could
significantly impact the organisation's strategic trajectory."

After (passes): "We found three things that should change the strategy."
